\begin{abstract}
Procedural Content Generation (PCG) is the automated content generation technique widely used in game development, urban planning, digital heritage, and educational simulation. It has become an increasingly beneficial tool in three-dimensional asset creation. Very few PCG technologies are built to support domain-specific environments like schools and universities. As such, this project proposes the development of a PCG system specifically for generating De La Salle University Manila (DLSU Manila) buildings, particularly those that are Neo-Classical in architectural style. This will be achieved through the creation of a C++ PCG 3D editor tailored for procedurally generating DLSU buildings and assets designed to replicate real-life structures.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Overview of Current Technology}
Procedural Content Generation (PCG) is the automatic generation of content through rules and constraints. It is a feature most commonly used in game development for its efficiency in terms of time consumption and labor. Using algorithms, developers are able to spawn or generate assets procedurally. PCG enables designers to create games with diverse environments and levels, which ultimately results in a more satisfying and immersive experience. It is employed to increase replay value, reduce production cost and effort, to save storage space, or simply as an aesthetic in itself \citep{summerville2017}.

In the context of games, titles like \emph{Minecraft} and \emph{Terraria} demonstrate the use of PCG to generate expansive, varied environments without manual design \citep{shaker2016}. This approach significantly reduces development time and resource demands while increasing replayability and adaptability \citep{lazaridis2024}.

PCG techniques are also being integrated into toolchains and wrappers compatible with 3D modeling and scene management software such as Maya, Blender, Unity, and Unreal Engine, allowing procedural methods to drive content creation pipelines. In architectural modeling and digital twin development, for instance, PCG is used to automate the generation of parametric building facades, road networks, and city layouts, which can then be exported into modeling environments for further refinement or simulation \citep{muller2006, parish2001}.

However, one documented challenge in current PCG applications—particularly in game environments—is the tendency for generated content to appear repetitive or generic, reducing the perceived authenticity of spaces like cities or architectural scenes \citep{togelius2013}.

Current research aims to address this issue, attempting to refine structural detail, variety, and cultural fidelity in procedurally generated models.

Beyond entertainment, PCG is gaining traction in areas such as urban planning, digital heritage, and educational simulation. Urban environments, much like virtual game worlds, require structured layouts and detailed asset placement, making them suitable for automated generation. Studies have shown that PCG can aid in generating realistic 3D cityscapes for planning, analysis, and visualization purposes, thereby supporting more efficient design workflows and enabling scenario testing in virtual simulations \citep{kim2018, parish2001}.

Academic venues such as SIGGRAPH, CASA, and the IEEE Conference on Games regularly publish advancements in PCG methods for urban modeling, cultural heritage reconstruction, and virtual tourism \citep{watson2008}.

For institutions such as universities, PCG offers a scalable way to replicate campuses in digital form, which can be used for educational tools, interactive simulations, and virtual tours. This presents opportunities across disciplines—from computer science and architecture to digital art and cultural studies—to explore generative systems both as research tools and practical applications for content creation. Aside from PCG, there is also already a mature body of software in the field of scene editors (e.g. Unity, Unreal Engine, Blender), most of them are built for general purposes, which involve manual creation of the assets. Very few of them are built to support PCG, specifically for domain-specific environments like schools/universities \citep{smith2011, togelius2016}.

As a solution, professionals have developed plug-ins that users can integrate into modern game engines or their own game engines, which allows the use of PCG techniques. These plug-ins are usually developed using languages like C++ and C\#, and are made as modular components.

\subsection{Statement of the Problem}
Due to the lack of PCG tools tailored for architectural modelling, this project aims to address the limitations of existing procedural content generation tools in accurately replicating neo-classical architecture. It seeks to develop a system that can generate accurate 3D models of DLSU buildings, with a focus on Neo-Classical design elements.

\subsection{Project Objectives}
The study's primary objective was to create a procedural content generation method suited for DLSU buildings. The 3D scene editor that implements this method will be named “Anito Builder.” The specific objectives of this project are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Analyze and identify the architectural style of DLSU buildings, with the aim of understanding the underlying design principles and techniques necessary for accurate replication.
    \item Create DLSU-themed building assets which includes both the modeling and texturing of the assets. 
    \item Create an art book, as evidence of the architectural study, that will showcase a selection of neoclassical architecture from both the real life and the game world as well as side-by-side comparison of the references and the recreated DLSU-themed buildings.
    \item Create a PCG method that can generate DLSU Architecture.
    \item Evaluate the created PCG method through a 3D scene editor with a series of qualitative and quantitative tests such as usability tests and quality tests.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Scope and Limitations}
Anito Builder primarily focused on procedurally generating the overall facade of Saint La Salle Hall at De La Salle University, Philippines. This included the creation of all exterior elements of the building, excluding all objects and structures found within the hallways and outside the building itself. It was accomplished by using neo-classical architecture as a reference, which is a type of architecture from the 18th century that emphasizes symmetry and grandeur. 

While the St. La Salle Hall originally held sophisticated and intricate details, the project focused on the overall structure and design of the building, rather than the finer details. This removed the need to create highly detailed models , which would have been time-consuming and inefficient considering the capabilities of the engine and the PCG method used.

As mentioned, the project revolved around the walls of La Salle Hall. As such, it was necessary to create the accompanying pillars, columns, windows, and anything that supports or encloses the wall. The project did not create models for any props like trash cans, food carts, and lamp posts since such objects are not part of the building. Instead, the focus will be on the structural parts that make up the building.

The models used in this project were made from scratch in Blender, since any engravings or statues were not covered due to legal concerns. The decision to use Blender is due to the extensive amount of plug-ins available for the program, increasing the speed and efficiency in creating 3D assets. Blender is a free software with precision modeling that is perfect for modeling architectures that require symmetry and proportionality. With the help of modeling features like array, subdivision, and deform modifiers, artists were able to produce highly-detailed models. Additionally, to develop further understanding of how assets, especially textures, behave when they are used for PCG methods, artists utilized current existing PCG plugins and built-in features for Blender during the modeling process.

For the textures, both Blender and Substance Painter were used as well as external programs that helped generate texture maps needed for integration into the Anito Builder. Substance Painter uses Physically-Based Rendering (PBR) which is good for simulating real-world lighting conditions. It depicts materials like marble and stone realistically. The PCG 3D editor was written in C++. This is because the underlying environment that the project was to be tested on is a game engine that is also written in C++. Furthermore, the BGFX rendering library to be used for the scene renderer is natively in C++. This project also utilized the CMake build system with the assistance of the VCPKG package manager.

\subsection{Significance of the Study}
Exploring DLSU buildings as a venue for PCG offers significant value. This project can serve as a reference on how to structure 3D modeling software for generalized use. It can provide developers with an idea of how to design their engine architecture if they are planning to create a 3D editor. Similarly, it will help 3D artists find a reference in understanding PCG and its application in urban modeling.

Additionally, procedural generation of universities or other similar institutions, specifically DLSU buildings, is uncommon. PCG-generated buildings and structures can be used as 3D assets for various activities such as advertisements, virtual tours, or simulations that can be showcased to various media.

Neoclassical architecture draws inspiration from the forms and ideals of ancient Greece and Rome, not simply for decorative purposes but to reflect a deeper cultural aspiration for order, rationality, and timeless beauty (Watkin, 2005). Central to this architectural movement is the use of classical orders—Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian—which provide a structured and proportioned system that guides the design of columns, entablatures, and facades (Summerson, 1980). These classical foundations support the monumental character often seen in neoclassical buildings, which are typically large in scale to convey a sense of power, permanence, and civic pride. This monumentality and grandeur made the style particularly fitting for government institutions, museums, and universities—spaces meant to embody collective ideals and stability (Bergdoll, 2000; Honour, 1968).

At the same time, neoclassicism is marked by a deliberate simplicity and restraint, distancing itself from the dramatic excesses of the Baroque. It favors clean lines, clear geometry, and measured ornamentation that reflect ideals of clarity and discipline (Watkin, 2005). These qualities align with the Enlightenment's emphasis on rationality and idealism, where structured, well-ordered spaces were thought to mirror and even promote the intellectual and moral progress of society (Summerson, 1980). Finally, the principles of symmetry and proportionality are fundamental to the style, with mathematically precise layouts that evoke harmony and balance. These design decisions were not purely aesthetic—they embodied a philosophical belief in the power of reason and the pursuit of ideal form (Summerson, 1980; Kruft, 1994).

Modern 3-dimensional modelling software (e.g., Blender, Houdini, Maya) offers tools for PCG (Hanif et. al., 2025, SideFX, n.d., Autodesk University, n.d.) These come in the form of plug-ins that other people have created, specific to work within that particular software (Mullane, 2007). Unreal Engine 5 is a modern Game engine that has procedurally generated content built into the engine. Unreal Engine 5 has many features that are meant to make games visually appealing. Many scenes become hard to run, and for the developers, it becomes hard to generate because their systems lack the speed necessary to run all these features.

\subsubsection{Ionic Order}
Unlike the Tuscan order, the Ionic order is fluted. It has a slender shaft and a height of approximately 8 times its diameter. Its capital has spiral scrolls (volutes) on either side and its entablature has a continuous frieze. It symbolizes femininity, grace, and elegance which supports the use of Ionic columns for the Temple of Athena Nike.

\subsubsection{Doric Order}
The doric order is a unique order in a sense that it does not have a base (see Figure 5). Although there is a base for it shown in the figure above, what is shown is a roman doric order, which has bases unlike greek doric bases that lack it. It has a fluted shaft and is significantly small in terms of proportions. Similar to the Tuscan order, it has a simple echinus and abacus and is meant to symbolize masculinity and strength. Its entablature commonly hosts triglyphs and metopes in the frieze. (Summerson, 1980). They are used for temples and civic buildings such as the Parthenon in Athens.

\subsubsection{Tuscan Order}
As illustrated in Figure 5, the Tuscan order is the simplest Roman order among all of them with an unfluted shaft as well as a simple base and capital. Its entablature is plain and minimally decorated. It lacks the complexity that the other orders have, and instead focuses on evoking a sense of strength and utility. They are commonly used for forts, warehouses, and military buildings.

\subsubsection{Corinthian Order}

The Corinthian order is the tallest and thinnest among all the classical orders with its height being 10 times its diameter. Its capital is decorated with acanthus leaves and scrolls; and its entablature is significantly more ornamented than Doric ones, similar to the Ionic order. It symbolizes luxury and beauty and is used for building interiors and monuments dedicated to gods. The most famous example of the Corinthian order is the Pantheon in Rome.

\subsubsection{Composite Order}
Similar to the Corinthian order, the Composite order is one of the orders with the tallest columns with its height being 10 times its diameter. Its capital is a combination of the Ionic and Corinthian styles, in a sense that it has both the volutes of the Ionic order and the acanthus leaves of the Corinthian order. It symbolizes grandeur and authority and is commonly used in arches and monuments.

\subsection{Concepts in Procedural Content Generation}
All procedural content generation methods involve an underlying formula. These formulas can be categorized based on its required inputs. Carli et. al (2011) categorizes PCG methods based on how much human intervention is needed in providing the inputs for the formulas. 

Low-human-intervention methods primarily encompass techniques such as noise-based generation and diffusion-based models. Noise-based methods are ones that use mathematical formulas such as Perlin or Simplex noise, to create organic, pseudo-random patterns. These methods are particularly effective for generating naturalistic textures, terrains, and distributions of elements, with human input largely confined to defining initial parameters and mapping noise values to desired features. Conversely, diffusion-based models, a prominent class of generative artificial intelligence, operate by progressively refining random noise into coherent data based on patterns learned from vast training datasets. While their initial training demands significant human effort in data curation and model architecture, the generation phase itself requires minimal input.

In contrast, high-human intervention methods are characterized by explicit human design that micro-manages content generation. One category of this is rule-based models. Rule-based models utilize human experience and knowledge to serve as the basis for pattern induction (i.e., rules) (Zhang, 2025). Rule-based models include shape grammars, L-systems, and cellular automata. Shape grammars are formal systems that apply transformative rules to geometric primitives, enabling the iterative generation of complex designs, commonly seen in architectural and urban planning. Lindenmayer Systems (L-Systems), employ recursive rewriting rules to model fractal structures and biological growth, notably used for realistic plant generation. Cellular automata involve grids of cells whose states evolve based on local rules, leading to emergent complexity suitable for simulating natural phenomena or generating abstract patterns.

Another category of high-human intervention methods is algorithmic procedural generation. This is a method wherein content is produced through a fixed, step-by-step process embedded within an algorithm. This approach relies on a pre-determined sequence of operations applied to specific inputs. This methodological choice prioritizes predictability and reliability.

\subsection{Concepts in Graphics Programming}

In the development of applications that require 3-dimensional visuals, the software needs to communicate with the Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) to render frames. This communication is managed by a Graphics Application Programming Interfaces (APIs).

Graphics APIs provide a standardized set of functions that allow developers to perform hardware-accelerated rendering and computational tasks on the GPU (Vulkan Working Group, 2026). Currently, the industry is defined by three primary standards: OpenGL, Vulkan, and DirectX 12 (DX12). OpenGL, maintained by the Khronos Group, has historically been the cross-platform standard, though its development has shifted toward maintenance as more modern alternatives emerge. Vulkan, also from the Khronos Group, released its 1.4 specification in early 2026, introducing the "Roadmap 2026" milestone to standardize advanced features like improved descriptor management and ray tracing across diverse hardware (Vulkan Working Group, 2026). Meanwhile, DirectX 12 remains the proprietary standard for Microsoft Windows and Xbox platforms, focusing on tight integration with the Windows operating system and high-performance gaming.

The primary distinction between these APIs lies in their level of abstraction, categorized as either high-level or low-level.

\begin{itemize}
	\item High-Level APIs (e.g., OpenGL): These APIs abstract away specific hardware details, such as memory allocation and CPU-GPU synchronization. This makes OpenGL easier for beginners to adopt but often results in higher CPU overhead and less predictable performance, as the driver makes many decisions on behalf of the developer (Yusov, 2025).
	\item Low-Level APIs (e.g., Vulkan, DX12): Often referred to as "explicit" APIs, these provide a thin abstraction layer that gives developers direct control over GPU resources. This allows for significant performance optimizations and better multi-threading capabilities, however, it requires the developer to manually manage complex tasks like memory barriers and command buffer submissions (Vulkan Working Group, 2026).
\end{itemize}

Modern game engines, such as Unity and Unreal Engine, act as an additional layer of abstraction above Graphics APIs. Rather than forcing developers to write API-specific code, these engines utilize an internal rendering abstraction layer. Unreal Engine, for example, uses its Render Hardware Interface (RHI) to translate engine-level commands into the appropriate Vulkan, DX12, or Metal calls depending on the target platform (N-iX Games, 2026). Unity employs a similar approach via its Scriptable Render Pipeline (SRP), allowing for a unified development workflow where the engine handles the underlying API integration automatically to ensure cross-platform compatibility (N-iX Games, 2026).

Two primary paradigms define how frames are rendered through these Graphics APIs:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Rasterization: The traditional method where triangles are projected onto a 2D plane. The GPU determines which pixels are covered by these triangles and colors themghly effici accordingly. It is hient and optimized for real-time performance but struggles with physically accurate reflections and shadows (Cable Matters, 2024).
	\item Raytracing: A method that simulates the physical behavior of light by casting rays from the camera into the scene to calculate intersections with objects. This produces photorealistic effects like global illumination and complex refractions but is computationally expensive, often requiring dedicated hardware acceleration found in modern GPUs (NVIDIA, 2020).
\end{itemize}
For projects that require more control than a full game engine but less boilerplate than raw Vulkan or DX12, developers often utilize Graphics API Wrappers. Libraries such as bgfx, Diligent Engine, and Wicked Engine provide a unified interface that wraps multiple back-end APIs into a single set of commands (Yusov, 2025).

\begin{itemize}
	\item Diligent Engine: A modular, cross-platform library designed to leverage the performance of most modern Graphics APIs like Vulkan and DX12 while maintaining support for legacy systems like OpenGL (Diligent Graphics, 2025).
	\item bgfx: A popular "bring your own engine" style library that focuses on being a cross-platform, API-agnostic rendering library.
\end{itemize}

These wrappers are almost exclusively written in C++ because the language provides the low-level memory access and deterministic performance required for high-performance graphics, while also offering the object-oriented features necessary to organize complex rendering pipelines (Yusov, 2025).

Integrating these C++ libraries into a project typically requires a build system, a set of tools that automates the compilation and linking of source code into an executable. For this purpose, CMake has become the standard build system for graphics programming (Kitware, 2026). Strictly speaking, CMake is a build system generator; it does not compile code itself but rather reads a CMakeLists.txt file to generate platform-specific project files, such as Visual Studio solutions on Windows or Makefiles on Linux (Viking Software, 2026). This allows graphics developers to maintain a single configuration file that works across different operating systems and hardware environments (Kitware, 2026).

\section{Methodology}

Anito Builder was created by a combination of artists and developer. The artists were be assigned to create the assets that would be imported to Anito Builder's local directory, while subsequently being used for the creation of the Art Bible. The developer of Anito Builder handled the creation of the internal logic, and also the subsequent 3D Scene Editor.

\subsection{3D Asset Development Workflow}

For PCG content, the 3D Asset workflows began with a creative direction. Artists and the software developer discuss what art style the assets should follow, followed by gathering references, making the mesh models, and then texturing the 3D assets. The chosen workflow has the following steps:

\subsubsection{Reference Gathering}

This step covered the gathering of images, from both online and real-life sources, that were used as references. Additionally, discussions and final decisions were made during this process to ensure a consistent art style.

\subsubsection{La Salle Hall Modularity Study}

To ensure that the 3D assets are optimized for procedural generation, the modularity of the La Salle Hall was studied. This involved analyzing the building's structure and design to identify repeating elements and patterns that could be efficiently modelled and reused in the PCG process. 

La Salle Hall has a very modular designs, with many repeating elements and patterns, making it a good candidate for procedural generation. Every level or floor of the building has a similar design, varying only in specific parts such as the center part of the building. With this in mind, it was only appropriate to model the assets in a way that allows for easy reuse and manipulation. 

For the first floor, the pattern was clear: arches and triple windows. The arches repeated for several segments, only getting interrupted by the windows. The second floor had a similar pattern, with the only difference being that the arches were replaced by rectangular windows. The third floor had a different pattern, with the windows being smaller and more closely spaced. The rooftop had a simple pattern, with the same wall repeating throughout the entire roof, only getting interrupted by the triangular pediment in the center. With all floors having a clear repeating pattern, it was easy to determine what to model.

\subsubsection{Mesh Modelling}

After ensuring that the modularity of the building is well-studied, the next step is to create the mesh models for the 3D assets.

In addition to low poly mesh optimization, areas with more detail that require high poly mesh modelling will be created during this step, making them separate models.
    
\subsubsection{Mesh optimization}

Once the initial meshes were created, optimizations and final passes were done to ensure that all the models have all their original features aside from the details and are functional and will run well on the engine and with the PCG method. This part of the process was one of the crucial steps in ensuring that the resulting mesh in the Anito Builder shows no issues. This step included checking for any mesh errors such as non-manifold edges, n-gons, z-depth fighting, and any other issues that arose after exporting them into the editor. 

A significant change that was made during this step was the decision to export the low-poly versions of the models instead of the subdivided and beveled ones due to the massive decrease in the performance of the editor when using high-poly models. Initially, the models were made with a high-poly mesh to capture all the details of the building such as the arches, bevels, and columns. However, when the engine was tested with the high-poly models, it was found that the performance of the engine was significantly decreased, making it difficult to test the PCG method. As such, the quality of the models was sacrificed for the sake of performance.

\subsubsection{UV Unwrapping}

Since the models were made to be repeated, it was important to ensure that the UVs of the models were properly unwrapped and optimized for tiling. Having a clean UV layout made sure that there were no texture issues like offsets, stretching, and seams when the models were used in the editor. 

\subsubsection{Texturing}

The texturing process involved three methods: procedural texturing in Blender, procedural texturing in Substance Painter, and taking real-life photographs of materials that can serve as textures for the models. 

Procedural texturing in Blender was done through the shader nodes. Blender has a very powerful procedural texturing system that allows artists to create complex textures without the need for image textures. By using a combination of noise textures, color ramps, bump maps, principled bsdf shaders, and other nodes, realistic textures were created for the models. To be able to import textures in the Anito Builder, a set of texture maps were needed, specifically the albedo, normal, and ARM maps. As such, these texture maps had to ba baked and exported from Blender, which did not turn out to be the most efficient and ideal method to go with.

The architectural framework of the Anito Builder 3D Editor is structured around a modular design that decouples resource acquisition from real-time state management. The system has two main branches: the Asset Loading system and Runtime Object Management.

The Asset Loading system implements a directory-crawling mechanism that identifies files based on supported types or extensions (e.g., fbx or obj files for geometric mesh data, jpg and png for textures). Upon identification, meta data is generated for these files before being passed to external libraries. In particular, data is processed through the Open Asset Import Library (Assimp), and texture data is processed through BGFX-BImg. Once processed, the handles of these resources are registered in their respective dictionaries. This ensures that assets are accessible by any system that requires them, and that assets are only loaded once unless the user requests a re-import.

Once assets are imported, the engine starts the Runtime Object Management system. This system is based on the node-hierarchy system by Godot. Wherein all objects in the scene inherit from a common base class.

\begin{itemize}
	\item Scene Integration: Each Object instance defines its spatial parameters through a local transformation matrix and its relative position within the scene hierarchy (parent-child relationships).
	
	\item Extensibility: The Object class acts as an interface for specialized functionality. Cameras, rigid-bodies, colliders, and renderers all derive from this base class. External libraries will also define their own child classes that inherit from the Object class.
	
	\item Interface and Manipulation: Each object exposes inspector data. In the Graphical User Interface (GUI) pass, a selected object's inspector data is retrieved and rendered in an inspector window. This interface is implemented using Dear ImGui, which interfaces with the bgfx graphics abstraction layer.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The PCG System}

The PCG system of Anito Builder is implemented as an external library. In the editor, it exists as a derived class of the base Object class, with specialized functionality and inspector data.

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.5\linewidth]{Figures/anitobuilder_arch.png}%
	\caption{Anito Builder PCG System Architecture}
\end{figure}

The PCG system works by creating a series of blocks that the user can transform. After creating these blocks, the walls are subdivided into smaller squares and scaled automatically. The algorithm and visual representation of this process is outlined below:

\begin{algorithm}
	\caption{Facade Wall Subdivision}
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
		\REQUIRE $H$ (Parent Height), $W$ (Parent Total Width)
		\REQUIRE $H_{max}$ (Max Wall Height), $W_{max}$ (Max Wall Width)
		\ENSURE A set of $N$ subdivided wall segments positioned in local space
		
		\STATE $n_{layers} \gets \lceil H / H_{max} \rceil$
		\STATE $n_{walls} \gets \lceil W / W_{max} \rceil$
		\STATE $S_y \gets 1.0 / n_{layers}$ \COMMENT{Local Y-scale (0 to 1 range)}
		\STATE $S_x \gets 2.0 / n_{walls}$  \COMMENT{Local X-scale (-1 to 1 range)}
		
		\STATE $Y_{start} \gets 0.0 + (S_y \cdot 0.5)$
		\STATE $Y_{end} \gets 1.0 - (S_y \cdot 0.5)$
		\STATE $X_{start} \gets -1.0 + (S_x \cdot 0.5)$
		\STATE $X_{end} \gets 1.0 - (S_x \cdot 0.5)$
		
		\FOR{$i \gets 0$ \TO $(n_{layers} \cdot n_{walls} - 1)$}
		\STATE $col \gets i \pmod{n_{walls}}$
		\STATE $row \gets \lfloor i / n_{walls} \rfloor$
		
		\STATE $t_x \gets (n_{walls} > 1) ? (col / (n_{walls} - 1)) : 0.5$
		\STATE $t_y \gets (n_{layers} > 1) ? (row / (n_{layers} - 1)) : 0.5$
		
		\STATE $P_x \gets {lerp}(X_{start}, X_{end}, t_x)$
		\STATE $P_y \gets {lerp}(Y_{start}, Y_{end}, t_y)$
		
		\STATE ${segment} \gets {InstantiateSegment}()$
		\STATE ${segment.position} \gets (P_x, P_y, 0)$
		\STATE ${segment.scale} \gets (S_x, S_y, 1.0)$
		\STATE ${StoreMetaData}({segment}, row, col)$
		\ENDFOR
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{figure}[H]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=1.0\linewidth]{Figures/stretchmethod.png}%
	\caption{Tiling and Stretching algorithm illustration}
\end{figure}

This system is based on editable blocks similar to what one can find on Unity's Probuilder or Blender's face edit mode. The user interface allows you to select any face of any particular block, and move it in the x-plane. While the cube's height can be controlled by dragging the roof up or down.

Furthermore, the cube can be extended by selecting a face and pressing "append" on the editor. By doing so, another cube will spawn with that selected face as a shared face between the old cube and the new cube. This can be done indefinitely to create various building shapes. This process is outlined visually below:

	\caption{Tiling and Stretching algorithm illustration}
\end{figure}

	\caption{Summary of inputs for the mesh choice algorithm}
	\begin{tabular}{|p{3cm}|p{10cm}|}
		\hline
		\textbf{Name} & \textbf{Description} \\
		\hline
		Column Index & Describes the current position of the segment in the X axis.\\
		\hline
		Floor Index & Describes the current position of the segment in the Y axis.\\
		\hline
		Face Width & Describes the total columns of the parent of the current segment.\\

Face selection as a method of shape manipulation was preferred over edge selection, which was the originally proposed plan for an older iteration of Anito Builder. Face selection was preferred because it was more intuitive that neoclassical buildings should follow a layout that is blocky or parallel by nature. Face translation through a wall's normal direction (i.e., the direction it is facing) makes it easier to create parallel lines in the layout.

We also conducted a System Usability Scale (SUS) test to assess the usability of the PCG-based 3D scene editor itself. The SUS is a widely adopted and validated tool in HCI research for quickly gauging overall system usability, and it provides a standardized score that can indicate whether the interface is efficient, intuitive, and accessible to users. This dual-evaluation strategy ensures that the study captures both the user’s visual and architectural impressions of the generated content and the functional usability of the editor that produces it.

\subsubsection{Evaluation of Anito Builder's output}

Similarly, the evaluation of the project’s output was gathered from students based in DLSU Manila. As DLSU Manila students are more familiar with La Salle Hall, it is only appropriate that participants are chosen from their population. The evaluation for Anito Builder’s output only includes renders from the final scene, which makes the need for asset development/programming knowledge unnecessary. 

After the participants are able to create scenes, they were given ample time to examine and explore the scene they have created. This scene served as the basis for their evaluation. They were then asked to answer a survey form adapted from instruments used by previous HCI studies such as Lavie and Taractinsky’s (2004) aesthetics model, Hassenzahl’s pragmatic and hedonic quality dimensions, and the Visual Aesthetic Perception (VAP) scale. On a scale of 1-5, the participants evaluated the scene based on this criteria: 

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Perceived Architectural Authenticity – measures how well the scene represents known DLSU architecture and its defining features (e.g., columns, facades, building symmetry).
    \item Stylistic Coherence – assesses whether visual elements appear consistent and aligned with a singular architectural style.
    \item Modular Variation and Design Flexibility – captures the user’s perception of how varied yet coherent the generated components are.
    \item Visual Appeal and Layout Clarity – evaluates subjective judgments of aesthetic quality, layout clarity, and overall pleasantness.
    \item Structural Believability – captures whether the forms look physically plausible and grounded in real-world architectural logic.
\end{enumerate}

Responses to the survey served as a means to provide insight into how effective the PCG system captures the look and feel of DLSU Manila. By making the evaluation focus solely on the output and not on the technical aspect of the development, it prioritizes user experience and visual quality. This is to make sure that the generated scenes meet the expectations of the participants in terms of architectural accuracy and design quality without needing any technical background.

\subsection{Asset Modularity Review}

With the nature of the LS Hall's architecture, it was easier to to create different types of modular wall segments than modelling the whole building itself. This is because the building has a pattern of wall segments that are used in different parts of the building. For example, the first floor of the LS Hall has a pattern of having arched hallways and the second floor has a pattern of having pillars and railings in equal segments. As such, the modular wall segments were created to replicate these patterns, and to be used in the PCG system to create the building.

wall segments are the basic building blocks of the PCG system. They are modular pieces with standardized dimensions cut out from the original building. By creating a library of these segments, the PCG system can efficiently generate new building layouts while maintaining architectural consistency. Each segment is designed to fit together seamlessly, allowing for a wide variety of configurations while adhering to the visual and structural language of the LS Hall.

The PCG method used by Anito Builder generates a set of wall segments based on the dimensions of the block that the user creates. Since a block consists of corners and faces, it was necessary to create models that would fit such method. This led to the creation of regular wall segments for the faces of the block, corner wall segments for the corners of the block, and rooftop segments for the roof of the block.

The regular wall and corner segments are the original segments that were modelled from the LS Hall. They are programmed to be used only in their respective floors (i.e., 1st floor segments can only be used in the first floor, and so on). The 2-segment and 3-segment models are models that were created to be used for larger faces. Unlike the previous segments, filler segments are modelled to fit all of the floors except for corners, and are based from both the La Salle Hall and Yuchengco Hall, which are the two buildings in the DLSU Manila campus with influences from neoclassical architecture.

The data collected during the structured task phase showed that workflow offered by Anito Builder was efficient. Results indicate that users progressively learned how to use the editor better throughout tasks (Table 2).

\begin{table}[h!]
	\centering
	\caption{Participant Performance Metrics per Task}
	\begin{tabular}{|p{3cm}|l|c|}
		\hline
		\textbf{Task \#} & \textbf{Target Structure} & \textbf{Mean Time (Min:Sec)} \\ \hline
		1 & La Salle Hall (Replica) & 5:31 \\ \hline
		2 & Cross-shaped Skyscraper & 4:16 \\ \hline
		3 & U-shaped Building & 3.56 \\ \hline
	\end{tabular}
\end{table}

As observed during Task 1, the majority of user interactions focused on learning the basic navigation of the 3D editor. Eventually, after users learned the basic structure of the interface, they required less assistance on the second task onward. Users who had a background using other 3D modeling software (i.e., blender) almost immediately knew how to navigate the editor. During tasks 2 and 3, users asked for assistance less, as all unfamiliar controls were already introduced to them during task 1.

The System Usability Scale (SUS) yielded a mean score of 78.5, which, according to Brooke (1996), places the Anito Builder in the ``Good'' to ``Excellent'' range of usability.

Post-experiment interviews indicated that the object-based hierarchy was intuitive for architectural layout, though some participants suggested that automatic snapping for the front and back wings would further reduce task time. Furthermore, when asked about their experience during the unstructured time, users noted their need of the following features:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item Roof removal — When the roof of a particular block is selected, a toggle to turn it off should exist.
	\item Automatic vertical placement — When the roof of a particular block is selected, a button to create a new block on top of that roof (that lines up perfectly with the source block) should exist.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

\subsection{Prototype Evaluation}

The prototype created is sufficiently equipped to act as a procedural asset generation tool at its current state. It can create environmental assets for games, or pieces of "filler" buildings in games that need much variation between similarly styled buildings. It can be used in city builders, driving simulators, or other types of games wherein the player cannot go into a building's interior.

Further improvements to the prototype include optimizations to the rendering pipeline. As there is observable framerate drops when rendering scenes with large blocks. Furthermore, to improve the viewing experience of users, some rendering features should be implemented. One of which is mipmapping, such that normal textures do not cause a model to appear too noisy when zoomed out. Another is ambient occlusion, such that the edges of geometry are darkened for visual clarity and realism.
